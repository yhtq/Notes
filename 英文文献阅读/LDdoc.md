Introduction
Include the following elements in brief:
● Purpose and Scope: Note the overall purpose of the literature review and define what the
review will and will not cover in relation to your research problem and questions.
● Organization of the Chapter: Indicate how the literature will be grouped (e.g., by
themes, historical trends, theoretical approaches) and why this structure is appropriate for
your study.
Research Questions: Include a brief restatement of your research questions to clarify
how they shaped the inclusion and arrangement of sources.
● Search Strategy: Outline the databases you used (e.g., ProQuest, JSTOR), your key
search terms, and your inclusion/exclusion criteria (e.g., publication date, peer-reviewed
status, relevance to topic).
● Source Requirements: Confirm that your sources are primarily peer-reviewed journal
articles. Non-scholarly sources such as dissertations, textbooks (e.g., Creswell), blogs,
.com sites, and books are not permitted.
● Thematic Preview: List the main themes or categories you will cover and briefly note
how they build toward your conceptual framework or study rationale.
Outline
Present the major themes and subthemes you have identified in the existing literature that
relate to your research problem and questions. Your outline should focus on synthesizing
ideas, not summarizing studies. Each theme should reflect a pattern or conversation in the
research and explain how this body of work connects to your study. Use bullet points or
brief paragraphs for each subtheme. Under each subtheme, include at least two peer-
reviewed sources and explain how they are connected, complementary, or contradictory,
and how they will support your literature review.
